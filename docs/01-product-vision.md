# Mini Budget Product Vision

## 1. Product Identity

Mini Budget is a private, offline-first financial recovery companion designed to help the user face their money honestly, reduce harmful spending habits, pay down debt, and build visible financial progress over time.

The app is not only a budget tracker. It is a motivational tool for financial recovery.

Mini Budget should make personal finance feel less scary, less condemning, and more actionable. The app should help the user take responsibility without feeling shamed when they fail, overspend, or fall behind.

## 2. Core Product Promise

> You may be in a difficult financial position, but you are not stuck. Log honestly, act weekly, fight your debts, and watch your future recover.

Mini Budget should help the user understand that progress is made through repeated small decisions. The app should turn daily discipline, debt payments, extra income, and honest tracking into visible recovery.

## 3. Primary User

Mini Budget is initially targeted at the creator of the app.

The primary user is:

- A young professional.
- Earning good money.
- Struggling with spending discipline.
- Carrying personal debt and a mortgage.
- Emotionally discouraged by their financial situation.
- Trying to take responsibility and make better decisions.
- Looking for encouragement, not condemnation.
- Wants a private local-first app instead of a cloud-controlled finance product.

The app should be built around the emotional reality of someone who is capable of improving their finances but currently feels overwhelmed, ashamed, or demotivated.

## 4. Emotional Design Principles

Mini Budget must feel:

- Encouraging.
- Honest.
- Calm.
- Supportive.
- Practical.
- Non-judgmental.
- Coach-like.
- Recovery-focused.

Mini Budget must not feel:

- Condemning.
- Harsh.
- Corporate.
- Overly complex.
- Like an accounting system.
- Like a bank app.
- Like a punishment tool.

The app should never shame the user for failure. It should acknowledge the situation and guide the next useful action.

Bad tone:

```txt
You failed your budget again.
```

Good tone:

```txt
This week went over plan, but you logged honestly. That gives you the information needed to recover next week.
```

Bad tone:

```txt
Your spending is irresponsible.
```

Good tone:

```txt
This category is putting pressure on your recovery plan. Reducing it by R300 this month would create more room for debt payments.
```

## 5. Core User Journey

The core emotional and financial loop is:

```txt
Face the truth
→ Make a weekly plan
→ Log honestly
→ See pressure early
→ Recover without shame
→ Pay debt
→ Watch net worth improve
→ Feel encouraged to continue
```

This loop should guide feature decisions.

Every major feature should support at least one part of this loop.

## 6. Product Pillars

## Pillar 1: Face Reality

The app helps the user look at their real financial position without panic.

Supported by:

- Expense logging.
- Expense categories.
- Income tracking.
- Debt tracking.
- Mortgage tracking.
- Net worth tracking.
- Summary dashboards.
- Monthly financial snapshots.

The app should make the current situation clear but not frightening.

Example message:

```txt
This is your current position. It is serious, but it is measurable, and measurable things can be improved.
```

## Pillar 2: Survive the Week

The app should make weekly spending easier to manage than abstract monthly budgets.

The weekly allowance system is one of the most important future features.

The app should help answer:

- How much can I safely spend this week?
- How much have I already spent?
- How many days are left?
- Am I spending too fast?
- What category is causing pressure?
- Can I still recover this week?

The weekly allowance is the user’s short-term discipline tool.

## Pillar 3: Defeat Debt

Debt should be treated as a first-class system, not just another expense category.

Debts should be represented as long-term enemies or bosses that the user fights over time.

Debt payments deal damage.
Interest can regenerate boss health.
Minimum payments prevent the boss from growing too quickly.
Extra payments create visible progress.

The user should be able to see:

- Total debt.
- Individual debt balances.
- Starting balance.
- Current balance.
- Interest rate.
- Minimum payment.
- Payment history.
- Debt-free projection.
- Strategy: snowball, avalanche, or hybrid.
- Monthly debt battle summary.

Debt progress must feel motivating even when the total debt is still large.

## Pillar 4: Build Net Worth

The user needs to see that they are becoming financially stronger, not merely surviving.

Net worth should be tracked as:

```txt
Net Worth = Assets - Debts
```

Assets may include:

- Bank balance.
- Emergency fund.
- Investments.
- Property value.
- Vehicle value.
- Other assets.

Debts may include:

- Credit cards.
- Personal loans.
- Store accounts.
- Mortgage.
- Debt review debts.
- Family or friend loans.

The app should show monthly movement.

Example:

```txt
Your net worth improved by R1,840 this month.
R1,200 came from debt reduction.
R640 came from increased savings.
```

This turns long-term recovery into visible progress.

## Pillar 5: Respect Extra Income

Extra income should be tracked separately because it represents sacrifice, effort, and opportunity.

The app should help the user see how additional work benefits their future.

Extra income may include:

- Overtime.
- Freelance work.
- Bonuses.
- Side projects.
- Once-off income.
- Reimbursements.
- Cash gifts.

The app should ask where extra income went:

- Debt payment.
- Emergency fund.
- Goal contribution.
- Living expenses.
- Personal reward.
- Other.

Example:

```txt
You earned R2,000 extra this month.
R1,500 went to debt.
That moved your debt-free date 3 weeks earlier.
```

This reinforces the relationship between short-term stress and long-term recovery.

## Pillar 6: Grow Through Goals

Goals should help the user move toward a better financial life.

The app should provide goal archetypes but allow full customization.

Goal archetypes:

- Emergency fund.
- Debt payoff.
- Phone, laptop, or car.
- Rent deposit.
- Holiday or travel.
- School fees.
- Home improvement.
- Investment.
- Custom goal.

Goal features:

- Target amount.
- Target date.
- Required monthly contribution.
- Feasibility status.
- Progress bar.
- Milestones.
- Rewards.
- What-if simulator.

The app should also gently guide whether a goal is healthy, neutral, or risky based on the user’s financial situation.

Healthy goals improve stability or recovery.
Neutral goals are fine when affordable.
Risky goals may slow recovery or increase pressure.

Example guidance:

```txt
This goal is possible, but it may slow your debt recovery. Consider lowering the monthly target or defeating your Credit Card boss first.
```

The app should not block user choice. It should inform and guide.

## 7. Gamification Philosophy

Gamification exists to support recovery, not distract from it.

The app should use game mechanics to make difficult financial work feel encouraging and visible.

Gamification should reward:

- Honesty.
- Consistency.
- Recovery.
- Debt payments.
- Reduced harmful spending.
- Extra income allocation.
- Weekly reviews.
- Backup discipline.

Gamification should not only reward perfection.

Important rule:

> Recovery actions should earn progress even after failure.

Example:

```txt
You overspent this week, but you logged everything and adjusted your next week plan. Recovery XP earned.
```

## 8. Core Game Systems

## Debt Bosses

Each debt can be represented as a boss.

Debt balance = boss HP.
Debt payments = damage.
Interest = regeneration.
Extra payments = bonus attacks.
Final payment = boss defeated.

Debt boss visuals should be motivating but not childish.

## Weekly Allowance Shield

The weekly allowance can be represented as a shield or health bar.

Spending reduces the shield.
Careful spending preserves the shield.
Recovering after overspending repairs the next plan.

The goal is not to shame the user for shield damage. The goal is to make spending pressure visible early.

## Discipline XP

XP should be awarded for useful financial behavior.

Possible XP events:

- Log expense.
- Complete daily check-in.
- Complete weekly review.
- Stay within weekly allowance.
- Pay a debt.
- Make an extra debt payment.
- Add extra income.
- Allocate extra income to recovery.
- Reduce spending in a weak category.
- Complete a no-spend day.
- Export or backup data.

## Recovery XP

Recovery XP is awarded when the user responds well after a bad week.

Examples:

- Logged all expenses despite overspending.
- Adjusted future allowance.
- Added a note explaining what happened.
- Reduced spending after an expensive day.
- Made a partial debt payment instead of skipping entirely.

This is critical to the emotional design of Mini Budget.

## Titles and Ranks

Possible rank names:

- Facing Reality.
- Budget Rookie.
- Expense Watcher.
- Allowance Defender.
- Debt Fighter.
- Habit Builder.
- Recovery Strategist.
- Net Worth Climber.
- Financial Guardian.

Ranks should reflect discipline, recovery, and consistency rather than income level or wealth.

## 9. Offline-First Principles

Mini Budget is local-first by default.

The app should support:

- Local-only data.
- No required account.
- No required internet.
- Export/import backup.
- PIN or biometric lock.
- CSV reports.
- PDF reports.
- Manual backup reminders.
- Optional local encryption.

The app should not include initially:

- Bank syncing.
- Shared household budgets.
- Automatic transaction imports.
- Cloud AI analysis.

Possible future online feature:

- Optional cloud account only for syncing between the user’s own devices.

Cloud sync must never become required for the core app experience.

## 10. Data Ownership Philosophy

The user owns their data.

The app should treat privacy as a product strength.

Core message:

```txt
Your financial recovery data stays in your hands.
```

The user should be able to:

- Use the app offline.
- Export their data.
- Import from backup.
- Delete their data.
- Protect the app with device security.

## 11. What Mini Budget Is Not

Mini Budget is not:

- A bank integration app.
- An accounting platform.
- A corporate finance dashboard.
- A shared household finance app.
- A cloud-first analytics product.
- A shame-based spending tracker.
- A replacement for professional financial advice.

Mini Budget is:

- A personal discipline tool.
- A private financial recovery companion.
- A motivational budgeting app.
- A debt-fighting assistant.
- A weekly spending control system.
- A long-term progress tracker.

## 12. Feature Priority

Recommended build order:

1. Product vision and Codex instructions.
2. Weekly allowance system.
3. Debt boss system.
4. Net worth tracking.
5. Extra income impact tracking.
6. Goals and feasibility guidance.
7. Gamification layer.
8. Privacy, backups, reports, and optional encryption.
9. Optional future cloud sync.

## 13. Decision Rule for New Features

Before adding a feature, ask:

1. Does this help the user face reality?
2. Does this help the user survive the week?
3. Does this help the user defeat debt?
4. Does this help the user see long-term progress?
5. Does this encourage recovery without shame?
6. Does this respect offline-first ownership?

If the answer is no to all of these, the feature probably does not belong in Mini Budget yet.
